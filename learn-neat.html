Quick guide

How NEAT and FoodSim work

FoodSim lets two teams of tiny agents race for fruit across the playfield. Their brains are trained with a slimmed-down version of NEAT (NeuroEvolution of Augmenting Topologies): a genetic algorithm that evolves neural networks by crossing and mutating the strongest candidates generation after generation.

What does NEAT mean in practice?

  • Genome: Every agent carries a small neural network that steers direction, avoidance and target choices.
  • Fitness: Collecting fruit and surviving longer awards more fitness points to the agent.
  • Selection: After each generation the most successful brains are kept and used as the base for the next wave.
  • Mutation: Tiny random tweaks to connections and weights inject variety that can uncover new strategies.

How the simulation is put together

In index.html both the simulation loop and the NEAT logic run directly in the browser. The centre canvas renders the world, while the surrounding panels provide real-time controls. Key pieces include:

  • Canvas: Repaints ~60 times per second with agent positions, trails, fruit and obstacles.
  • Simulator: Tracks collisions, scoring and the step counter for the current generation.
  • Genetics: When a generation ends, agents are ranked, elites are copied forward and the rest are rebuilt through mutation and crossover.
  • UI bindings: The sliders on the side panels feed directly into script variables to tune the run on the fly.

Which controls make the biggest difference?

Experiment with the sliders on the main page. The most impactful settings are:

  • Steps / gen: More steps give agents extra time to explore before the next generation starts.
  • Mutation %: Higher mutation introduces randomness that can discover fresh ideas but may feel erratic.
  • Elite ratio: Decides how many top brains carry over untouched to the next generation.
  • Team parameters: Speed, awareness, strategy and separation shape how quickly agents move, how far they sense and how tightly they cluster.
  • Global controls: Fruit count, obstacle density and trail length all create new environments to adapt to.
Tip: Increase mutation and lower the elite ratio if the population gets stuck in a dull routine. Reduce mutation and raise the elite ratio to stabilise a promising behaviour pattern.

Ready to dive deeper?

Check the JavaScript at the end of index.html to see how neurons and their connections are defined. It is also the place to add more sensory inputs or adjust the fitness formula when you want to extend the experiment.
